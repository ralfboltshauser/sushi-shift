## Style

This is a stylized low-poly / low-detail 3D pack with a warm, toy-like look. It uses soft proportions, simplified geometry, bright atlas textures, and readable silhouettes rather than realism. The restaurant setting is Japanese sushi themed: shoji walls, red lanterns, bamboo, torii gate, sushi bar counters, bowls, ramen, nigiri, and chibi animal characters.

The style is a good fit for:

- Cozy cooking sim
- Overcooked-style kitchen chaos
- Sushi restaurant management game
- Tiny diorama restaurant builder
- Casual customer-service game
- Multiplayer local co-op prototype

It is not a good fit for realistic cooking, photorealistic kitchens, gritty survival crafting, or serious restaurant simulation without replacing the visuals.

## Formats

The pack ships in:

- Blender source files: `.blend`
- Unity/Unreal-friendly interchange: `.fbx`
- Godot-friendly interchange: `.gltf`
- OBJ/MTL fallback: `.obj` and `.mtl`

For Godot 4, start with the `.gltf` files.

## Practical Read

The pack is strongest as a playable prototype kit, not just a prop library. It gives you:

- Characters with cooking-relevant animations
- Tool-holding character variants
- Modular kitchen and restaurant construction pieces
- Finished dishes
- Raw ingredients
- Decorative Japanese restaurant flavor

The biggest gameplay gap is that the pack does not seem to include many explicit intermediate cooking states for each ingredient, such as chopped avocado, burnt fish, plated half-complete recipes, or dirty dishes. You can still fake a lot of that by swapping between ingredient props and finished dish props.

Best first prototype: a small sushi counter game where the player takes orders, grabs ingredients, chops fish/cucumber, assembles rolls/nigiri, cooks pan items, and serves customers before patience runs out.
